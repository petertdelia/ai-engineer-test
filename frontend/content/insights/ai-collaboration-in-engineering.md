Good engineers don't treat an AI assistant like a search engine. They treat it like a smart colleague: ask targeted questions, evaluate the response critically, and push back when something doesn't add up.

## The three failure modes

**Blind trust.** The engineer accepts every AI suggestion without verifying it against their own understanding. The code compiles; the logic is wrong.

**Learned helplessness.** The engineer prompts the AI to solve the problem wholesale and waits. The assistant refuses to give a direct answer. The engineer prompts again, in a different way. Time passes.

**Stubborn avoidance.** The engineer uses the AI so rarely, or so superficially, that their score on AI Collaboration is essentially zero. In a world where the tools exist, not using them is its own signal.

## What high-scoring candidates do

They use the assistant early — not to get the answer, but to orient themselves. "What are the key failure modes for this kind of database query?" is a better opening than "Fix my code."

They iterate. A good AI interaction has three to five exchanges. The candidate proposes an approach, the assistant identifies a gap, the candidate refines their thinking. The final answer is theirs.

They calibrate. When the assistant suggests something that conflicts with their knowledge, they note it, test their assumption, and either update their view or explicitly disagree in their response. Both are valid.
